## Working Papers

### Exploring Autocrats' Toolkit Against the Population Decline

How do authoritarian governments ensure compliance with state objectives when traditional tools of coercion may be ineffective? This paper examines how autocratic regimes adapt their political control strategies in response to demographic crises, with a focus on the recent turn toward pro-natalism. While coercion has historically been a hallmark of authoritarian governance, the moral, logistical, and political costs of enforcing childbearing compel states to adopt alternative methods. I argue that authoritarian governments shift beyond coercion or indoctrination toward the distribution of incentives as the primary strategy to motivate individual compliance. Leveraging government-controlled newspaper content from China and Singapore, this study analyzes how autocratic states adopt different political control strategies to foster compliance towards fertility differently in population control and population boosting periods. Time series analysis finds that incentive-based approaches and gender-progressive rhetoric are used more to encourage births. In doing so, the paper contributes to theories of authoritarian political control, distribution, and state governance of gender.

### The Electoral Consequences of Abortion Bans

Do abrupt losses in abortion access shift partisan support at the ballot box? This question became urgent after the Supreme Court’s Dobbs v. Jackson Women’s Health Organization (2022) overturned Roe v. Wade and enabled sweeping state-level restrictions. Abortion is both a critical health service and a central partisan cleavage, yet we lack evidence on how sudden reductions in access translate into electoral outcomes. I argue that inaccessibility of abortion reshapes partisan preferences by altering how voters experience and attribute responsibility for this policy-driven hardship. To test this claim, I use a difference-in-differences design leveraging geographic variation in county-level distances to abortion services facing post-Dobbs clinic closures. Results show that the Republican Party is electorally punished in 2022 at the gubernatorial level but rewarded in the 2024 Presidential election, pointing to context-dependent electoral effects. The study contributes to research on gender politics and electoral behavior, showing that abortion’s political impact is counterintuitive and complex.
